## Supratherapeutic INR without bleeding

For an INR above the therapeutic range but below 4.5 without bleeding,
holding one or two warfarin doses and resuming at a lower dose is usually
sufficient. For an INR between 4.5 and 10 without bleeding, hold warfarin;
routine vitamin K is not required. For an INR above 10 without bleeding,
give oral vitamin K (2.5–5 mg) in addition to holding warfarin. Recheck the
INR daily and look for the cause of the excursion — new drug interactions,
acute illness, or dietary change.

## Major bleeding on warfarin

For major or life-threatening bleeding, stop warfarin and give four-factor
prothrombin complex concentrate (4F-PCC) together with 5–10 mg of
intravenous vitamin K. 4F-PCC corrects the INR within minutes and is
preferred over fresh frozen plasma, which requires large volumes and slower
infusion. Because vitamin K takes 12–24 hours to restore endogenous factor
synthesis, both agents are needed: the concentrate for immediate hemostasis
and vitamin K to prevent rebound anticoagulation.

## Periprocedural management

For most procedures, stop warfarin about five days beforehand and confirm
the INR is below 1.5 (or the proceduralist's threshold) on the day of
surgery. Reserve bridging with therapeutic low-molecular-weight heparin for
patients at highest thrombotic risk — for example, a mechanical mitral
valve, recent venous thromboembolism within three months, or severe
thrombophilia; routine bridging for atrial fibrillation increases bleeding
without preventing stroke. Minor dental, dermatologic, and cataract
procedures can usually proceed without interrupting warfarin.

## Restarting anticoagulation

After bleeding is controlled, reassess the original indication and restart
anticoagulation when the thrombotic risk again outweighs the rebleeding
risk — often within one to two weeks for gastrointestinal bleeding in
patients with strong indications such as mechanical valves or recent
thromboembolism. Document the reversal agents given, the achieved INR, and
the restart plan in the discharge summary, and arrange early INR follow-up
because sensitivity to warfarin is frequently altered after acute illness.
