## Definition and staging

Acute kidney injury is diagnosed by a rise in serum creatinine of 0.3 mg/dL
or more within 48 hours, a rise to 1.5 times baseline within seven days, or
urine output below 0.5 mL/kg/hour for six hours. Severity is staged 1–3 on
the magnitude of the creatinine rise and the depth and duration of
oliguria; stage 3 includes a creatinine of 4.0 mg/dL or more or initiation
of renal replacement therapy. Trend the creatinine against the patient's
true baseline, not the admission value — a "normal" creatinine can already
represent a doubling in a patient with low muscle mass.

## Initial evaluation

Classify the injury as prerenal, intrinsic, or postrenal. Review volume
status, blood pressure trends, and the medication list; examine the urine
sediment; and obtain a bladder scan or renal ultrasound when obstruction is
possible. Urine studies (sodium, creatinine, fractional excretion of sodium
or urea) help distinguish prerenal physiology from established tubular
injury, but they are confounded by diuretics and chronic kidney disease.
Muddy brown granular casts point to acute tubular necrosis; dysmorphic red
cells or red-cell casts should raise concern for glomerulonephritis and
prompt urgent nephrology input.

## Supportive management

Restore perfusion — treat hypovolemia with crystalloid and hold
antihypertensives when appropriate — and treat the underlying cause. Dose
all renally cleared medications to the current (not baseline) kidney
function, and reassess daily as the creatinine moves. Avoid routine
diuretics except to manage volume overload, and do not use low-dose
dopamine, which has no renal benefit. In oliguric patients, track a strict
intake/output record and daily weights.

## Nephrotoxin stewardship

Stop or avoid nephrotoxins: NSAIDs, aminoglycosides, amphotericin, and the
combination of vancomycin with piperacillin–tazobactam carry particular
risk. Hold renin–angiotensin blockers (ACE inhibitors, ARBs) and consider
holding SGLT2 inhibitors during the acute episode. Iodinated contrast
should not be withheld when a study is genuinely needed, but volume
expansion beforehand is reasonable in high-risk patients, and unnecessary
contrast exposures should be deferred.

## Indications for renal replacement

Urgent dialysis is indicated for refractory hyperkalemia, severe acidosis,
volume overload unresponsive to diuretics, uremic complications
(pericarditis, encephalopathy), and certain dialyzable intoxications — the
classic AEIOU mnemonic. Absent an urgent indication, timing is guided by
trajectory rather than a creatinine threshold; early initiation without an
urgent indication has not improved outcomes. Involve nephrology early for
stage 3 injury, an unclear cause, or anticipated renal replacement.
