## Diagnostic criteria

Diabetic ketoacidosis is defined by the triad of hyperglycemia (glucose
typically above 250 mg/dL), ketonemia or significant ketonuria, and a
high-anion-gap metabolic acidosis (arterial pH below 7.30 or serum
bicarbonate below 18 mEq/L). Euglycemic DKA can occur with SGLT2-inhibitor
use, in pregnancy, or after partial insulin dosing, so a near-normal glucose
does not exclude the diagnosis when the anion gap is elevated and ketones
are present. Check a basic metabolic panel, beta-hydroxybutyrate, and a
venous blood gas at presentation, and search for the precipitant —
infection, ischemia, missed insulin, or a new medication.

## Fluid therapy

Begin resuscitation with isotonic crystalloid, typically 15–20 mL/kg (about
1–1.5 L) over the first hour, then adjust the rate and tonicity to the
corrected sodium and hemodynamic response. When the glucose falls below
about 200 mg/dL, add dextrose to the intravenous fluids so the insulin
infusion can continue until the ketoacidosis clears. Balanced crystalloids
are a reasonable alternative to normal saline and may reduce
hyperchloremic acidosis with large-volume resuscitation.

## Insulin therapy

Start a continuous intravenous regular-insulin infusion at approximately
0.1 units/kg/hour (an initial 0.1 units/kg bolus is optional). Do not start
insulin until the serum potassium is at least 3.3 mEq/L, because insulin
drives potassium intracellularly and can precipitate a dangerous
hypokalemia. Target a glucose decline of roughly 50–75 mg/dL per hour, and
continue the infusion until the anion gap has closed — glucose
normalization alone does not mean the DKA has resolved.

## Potassium management

Total-body potassium is depleted in DKA even when the measured serum value
is normal or high. If potassium is below 3.3 mEq/L, hold insulin and replete
first; between 3.3 and 5.2 mEq/L, add 20–30 mEq of potassium to each liter
of intravenous fluid; above 5.2 mEq/L, withhold supplementation and recheck
every two hours. Monitor electrolytes every two to four hours during the
acute phase, and remember that phosphate and magnesium commonly fall as
well.

## Transition to subcutaneous insulin

Transition to subcutaneous insulin only when the patient can eat and the
DKA has biochemically resolved: glucose below 200 mg/dL plus two of the
following — anion gap 12 or less, bicarbonate 15 mEq/L or greater, and
venous pH above 7.30. Give the first dose of basal insulin one to two hours
before stopping the infusion; an insulin-naive patient can usually start at
0.5–0.8 units/kg/day divided between basal and prandial coverage.
Discontinuing the infusion without overlapping basal insulin is a common
cause of rebound ketoacidosis.
