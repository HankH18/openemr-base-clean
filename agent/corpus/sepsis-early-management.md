## Recognition and screening

Suspect sepsis in any patient with a proven or suspected infection plus new
organ dysfunction — hypotension, altered mentation, oliguria, hypoxemia, or
a rising lactate. Screening scores (qSOFA, SIRS, NEWS) support but do not
replace clinical judgment; a normal score never excludes sepsis in a
patient who looks ill. Septic shock is sepsis with persistent hypotension
requiring vasopressors to keep the mean arterial pressure at 65 mmHg or
above and a lactate above 2 mmol/L despite adequate fluid resuscitation.

## Initial resuscitation

For sepsis-induced hypoperfusion or septic shock, give 30 mL/kg of
intravenous balanced crystalloid within the first three hours, then guide
further fluid by dynamic measures of responsiveness (passive leg raise,
pulse-pressure variation) rather than fixed volumes. Obtain blood cultures
before antibiotics whenever that does not materially delay therapy, and
measure a lactate at presentation. Reassess perfusion frequently —
capillary refill, urine output, mentation, and repeat lactate.

## Empiric antimicrobials

In septic shock, or when sepsis is probable, give broad-spectrum
antimicrobials within one hour of recognition; each hour of delay in shock
increases mortality. Choose coverage based on the suspected source, local
resistance patterns, recent antibiotic exposure, and risk factors for
resistant organisms such as MRSA or Pseudomonas. Control the source —
drain abscesses, remove infected catheters, address obstructed biliary or
urinary tracts — as early as is feasible.

## Lactate and reassessment

When the initial lactate is elevated (above 2 mmol/L), remeasure it within
two to four hours and use lactate clearance as one marker of resuscitation
adequacy. A persistently rising lactate despite fluids and antimicrobials
should prompt reassessment for uncontrolled source, inadequate perfusion
pressure, or an alternative diagnosis. Document a repeat focused exam after
every resuscitation phase — the reassessment, not the initial bundle, is
where deterioration is usually caught.

## Vasopressors and MAP target

Start norepinephrine as the first-line vasopressor when hypotension
persists despite initial fluid resuscitation, targeting a mean arterial
pressure of at least 65 mmHg. Add vasopressin as a second agent rather than
escalating norepinephrine indefinitely, and consider hydrocortisone for
shock refractory to combined pressors. Vasopressors may be started through
good peripheral access while central access is being arranged — do not
delay perfusion pressure for a line.
